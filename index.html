01 — Foundation

Executive Summary

What Sorcerer Survivors is, who it's for, and exactly how it beats the games it will be compared to.

Sorcerer Survivors is a dark-fantasy co-op bullet-heaven survivor for 1–4 players on Roblox. Every player is a sorcerer of one of two primordial magics — the Radiant Order (living light of the first sunrise) or the Shadow Covenant (living shadow from the spaces between stars) — surviving endless tides of nightmare creatures. Like Magic Survival, the player's only direct control is movement (plus a class dash); all spells cast automatically. Every sorcerer studies from six shared schools — four elemental, two arts of magic — plus their faction's special school (Holy or Void), which carries its risk engine. The depth emerges from spell selection, spatial placement on the shared party Web, cross-player spell fusion, artifact building, and the twin risk-engines of the Curse and Radiance systems.

What no competitor offers: faction asymmetry that makes co-op the fantasy itself. Shadow sorcerers naturally soak the tide and grow from suffering; light sorcerers mend, cleanse, and burn. The crown mechanic — the Eclipse spell, fused across two players from both factions and cast from both simultaneously — is the genre's first truly cross-player build mechanic.

Target Audience

Roblox core (10–18) and survivor-genre players. Fans of Magic Survival, Vampire Survivors, and The Spell Brigade. Readable in 5 seconds, mastered over 100 hours per faction — and there are two factions.

Unique Selling Points

Cross-player Eclipse spell fusion · One shared party Web — co-op build-crafting on a single board · Two-faction split with fully separate profiles ("two games in one") · Twin Curse/Radiance risk meters carried by each faction's special school · Pact deals with persistent entity memory · Free-to-play on the largest social games platform

Steam made "Bullet Heaven" an official genre tag in May 2026 — the genre is mature and crowded on PC, but nearly empty on Roblox, where multiplayer, social discovery, and mobile-native controls are the platform's default strengths. Sorcerer Survivors is positioned as the genre's definitive co-op entry where the co-op audience already lives.

02 — Foundation

Design Pillars

Five principles every design decision is measured against. The core fantasy in one line: "You are one half of an eclipse."

I
Movement Is the Only Verb

One stick and a dash. Every spell casts itself. All depth lives in what you learn, where you place it, and what you sacrifice for it. A six-year-old can survive the first run; a veteran reads the whole screen like sheet music.

II
Power Is Always a Trade

Curse trades safety for damage. Radiance trades anonymity for strength. Fusion trades build width for peak power. Pacts trade something meaningful, always. There is no free power past the early game — and players learn to love the price.

III
Two Halves, One Survival

Faction asymmetry creates natural co-op roles without rigid role-locks. Shadow draws and endures the tide; light mends and purges it. Every cross-faction interaction — cleanse, peel, Eclipse — is a designed moment of positive coupling. Cooperation makes you stronger, never just safer.

IV
The Build Is a Place, Not a List

The hexagonal Web turns every level-up into a spatial decision. Adjacency creates resonance; geometry creates dilemmas; fusion scars the grid. In co-op the whole party shares one board — your neighbor's spells are literally your neighbors. Two runs with identical spells play entirely differently.

V
Lore Whispers, Never Lectures

No forced cutscenes, ever. The world is delivered through flavor text, visual design, entity dialogue, and what the Pacts want from you. Players who read construct the full picture; players who skip still understand the world intuitively. The mystery is content.

03 — Foundation

World Lore & Fantasy Setting

The mythology that gives every mechanic its meaning — delivered only in whispers.

The First Sunrise

Before the world had a sky, there was only the dark between stars — patient, complete, and home to things that thought slowly and lived forever. Then came the First Sunrise: not a dawn, but an arrival. Light landed in the dark the way a stone lands in still water. The Radiant Order remembers it as salvation. The Shadow Covenant remembers what it displaced.

Where light and dark met and neither yielded, the world tore. The tear never healed. It dreams. Its dreams are the Nightmare Tide — creatures that are neither light nor shadow but the wound between them, pouring out each night to extinguish whatever still chooses a side.

The Two Orders

The Radiant Order are sorcerers of living light, drawing power from the first sunrise: ancient, purifying, relentless. The Shadow Covenant are sorcerers of living shadow, drawing power from the spaces between stars: patient, consuming, recursive. They have been rivals for a thousand years — and they are the only two things the Tide cannot dream its way around. Every run is a night spent holding the line, and every Eclipse — two mastered spells fused across the divide — stitches one thread of the wound closed. Neither order will admit this is why they keep fighting side by side.

The Entities

Older than both orders are the Pact Entities — beings who survived the First Sunrise by becoming something the wound cannot touch: pure appetite, pure commerce, pure patience. They appear mid-run with deals. They are not evil. They are exact.

Lore Design Note

Lore is delivered via spell and relic flavor text, Pact Entity dialogue, zone environmental design, and boss name-plaques — never forced cutscenes. Entities remember your choices across runs and their dialogue evolves; repetition is the storytelling instrument. Players who read flavor text construct the full picture; those who skip it still understand the world intuitively from visual design.

05 — The Player

Controls & Movement

Magic Survival's single-input purity, adapted for Roblox's input diversity. Movement is survival; everything else is automatic.

Mobile / Tablet (majority of Roblox)

  • Virtual stick — left thumb, drag-to-place anywhere
  • Dash button — bottom-right; class-specific dash on 3–5s cooldown
  • All casting automatic; zero aim inputs
  • Level-up: tap a card; Web placement: tap suggested slot or drag to override
  • Pact / fusion prompts: full-screen cards with large accept/decline

PC / Console

  • WASD / left stick — 8-directional movement with slight momentum
  • Shift / Space / trigger — dash
  • Mouse or stick selects cards; Escape pauses (Web view)
  • Ping wheel (hold Tab / d-pad): "Cleanse me" · "Altar here" · "Fuse?" · "Group up" — co-op language without voice chat

Movement Feel

Responsive, physics-lite, slight momentum — fast enough to thread tight corridors of enemies, never floaty. Dash is each class's signature expression (a blink, a plow, a phase) and the player's only active button, so it must feel deliberate and loud. Spell shapes (orbit, lance, zone, swarm) are designed to reward specific movement styles, making positioning the real skill ceiling.

Design Reference: Magic Survival

Magic Survival's brilliance is the purity of one input and zero attack decisions. Sorcerer Survivors preserves this and adds exactly one button (dash) and one social layer (pings). Nothing else competes for the player's hands.

The Two Orders & Core Stats

At first launch the player chooses a side. Each side is a fully separate profile — switching sides later opens a second, fresh grimoire, deliberately doubling the game.

The School Structure

Faction identity no longer comes from which magic you can learn — it comes from the one school no one else can touch, the risk engine it carries, and how your half of the eclipse plays. A Radiant pyromancer and an Shadow pyromancer cast the same Emberlance; only one of them can consecrate the ground it burns.

Combat & Status Effects

The full damage pipeline, every status effect, the threat system, and what happens when you fall. One page that answers every "but how does X actually work?"

The Damage Pipeline

FinalDamage = Base(L) × (1 + Potency) × MeterMult × (1 + Resonance) × (1 + Amps) × Crit

Base(L) : the spell's level-scaled damage (§20 curve)
Potency : sum of all artifact/research/pact % bonuses (additive within category)
MeterMult : Curse or Radiance stage multiplier (1.0 if not enrolled)
Resonance : sum of resonance damage bonuses on that spell
Amps : sum of external amplifiers on the TARGET (Fungal Brand, Heart of the Glacier, etc.)
Crit : ×2.0 on a 5% roll (modified by relics/talents)
  • One rule: bonuses add within a category, categories multiply. Designers and players only memorize this once
  • Crits roll per damage instance. DoTs and auras never crit (unless a Talent explicitly says so) — keeps tick damage predictable
  • Enemy armor (Elites/bosses only): damage taken = dmg × 100 / (100 + armor). Entropy Field and Unravel strip it
  • Target caps: a single enemy can be hit by the same spell instance once per 0.5s — no shotgun-stacking one frame of overlapping hits

Stagger — making CC matter on bosses

Wardens and Sovereigns are immune to Freeze, Stun, and Knockback — but every blocked CC effect fills a visible Stagger ring under their health bar. When full: a 3-second Break (boss stunned, takes +25% damage), then the ring resets and its capacity grows 25% (capped at +150% of base after repeated Breaks). If no CC is blocked for 8s the ring decays 5%/s — so Stagger rewards a real control build rather than trickling full over a long fight. Control builds stay relevant in boss fights without trivializing them, and the party gets a shared burst-window to coordinate around.

Threat — who the tide hunts

Threat(enemy → player) = 100 + RadianceAggro − (0.5 × distance in studs) + recentDamageBonus
RadianceAggro : 0 / +10 / +25 / +50 / +100 by Radiance stage (Stage V: targets you regardless)
Taunts : Shadow Colossus and Shade Hound taunts override targeting for their duration
Exceptions : Lightdrinkers ignore distance; Tidecallers never move

Enemies re-evaluate targets every 2 seconds — recent enough to feel responsive, slow enough that peeling an ally visibly works.

Getting Hit, Going Down

  • Hit protection: 0.4s of i-frames after taking contact damage. This caps incoming hits at ~2.5/s — which is what makes Curse's fixed +4/hit fair and predictable
  • Downed: at 0 HP you become a tethered spirit drifting with the party. Bleed-out timer: 90s, −20s per subsequent down (min 30s)
  • Revives — the shared pool: a normal run hands the party a Revive Pool equal to the number of players (solo 1 · duo 2 · trio 3 · quad 4) — the run's entire reserve of extra lives. Every revive spends one charge: an ally channel (3s within 6 studs; 1.5s if two allies channel the same spirit → back at 40% HP), a solo bleed-out auto-revive (25% HP at timer expiry), Phoenix Sigil's Rebirth Pact, and Radiance Vigil (instant, 50 Radiance) all draw from it. When the pool is empty, every down is final — no normal run has unlimited lives. (The Drown is the run's high-density final stretch, §17.)
  • Solo: with no ally to channel, your single pool charge spends as one bleed-out auto-revive; after that, the next down ends the run (two lives in total). Solo is tuned around not getting hit; co-op around spending the pool wisely
  • Dash: 0.3s of dodge i-frames on every class dash (0.4s with the research node). The skill expression for surviving the Drown
09 — Magic Systems

The Spell Codex

32 spells across 8 schools. Six schools are open to every sorcerer — four that study the elements (Fire, Ice, Lightning, Nature) and two that study magic itself (Arcane, Summoning) — plus each faction's special school: Holy ☀ for the Radiant Order, Void ☾ for the Shadow Covenant. Each spell levels 1→10 with behavior milestones at Lv4 and Lv7. At Level 10, the player chooses one of three Talent paths that fundamentally reshapes the spell ("Mastery"). Inspired by Magic Survival's 21-spell system, with deeper evolution branching.

Spell Anatomy

Lv 1–9: logarithmic numeric growth (§20) plus a felt behavior jump at Lv4 and Lv7 (+projectile, pierce, larger zone). Lv 10: choose 1 of 3 Talents — the spell is renamed, re-skinned, re-shaped, and becomes Mastered (glows on the Web; prerequisite for Fusion). Tags drive Resonance and fusion recipes.

The Web & Resonance

A hexagonal honeycomb of soul-slots — the game's signature build system. Your build is a place, not a list. And in co-op, it is a shared place: the whole party weaves on one board.

One Web, Whole Party

Solo, the Web is your personal rosette. In co-op, the rosettes interlock directly into one continuous honeycomb — no gaps, no neutral filler: every player owns a region of cells, and where regions border each other, the shared edges form seams. You place spells only in your own region — but spells adjacent across a seam resonate exactly like neighbors on your own board. Your ally's spells are literally your neighbors, and "who takes the seam cell" becomes the party's favorite argument.

Rules

  • Each sorcerer's region is a rosette of 6 hexagonal cells ringing its empty center — the Heart Gap
  • The Heart Gap: a Heart Gap can only be filled by an allied Wide Soul sorcerer — never by its owner. Solo, it stays empty (the Locket's phantom seam manifests there). A spell placed in an ally's Heart Gap is ringed by all six of that ally's cells — the strongest resonance position on the board, reserved for the support archetype
  • The Wide Soul users: Wide Soul classes carry a spell capacity of 8, but their rosette still has only 6 cells. A Wide Soul sorcerer can only fill all 8 of their slots within their 6 hexagonal cells by combining spells — each fused cell counts as two spells toward capacity, so reaching 8 requires two fusions (4 single cells + 2 fused cells = 8 spells in 6 cells). Allies' Heart Gaps give them extra placement room beyond their rosette, but the 8-in-6 itself is achievable only through fusion
  • On choosing a spell at level-up, the player chooses which empty cell in their region it occupies; the level-up card previews every Resonance each placement would activate — including seam resonances with allies' spells
  • Placement is a commitment — repositioning is possible only at Pact altars or via specific relics
  • Adjacent spells resonate, within a region or across a seam; a spell can resonate with multiple neighbors simultaneously. Seam resonances apply at full strength — each effect to its owner's spell
  • Fusion (§11) collapses two cells into one. For standard classes the donor cell scars (capacity −1 for the run) — the Web literally remembers. For Wide Soul classes fusion compresses instead of scarring: the freed cell stays usable, which is precisely how they reach 8
  • Artifacts never occupy cells; they are worn, not woven
  • A player leaving mid-run undocks their region; seam resonances with it fade over 30s, and any spell they held in an ally's Heart Gap fades with them

Attunement & Eclipse — The Combination System

The late-run payoff. Two Mastered (Lv10 + Talent) spells, adjacent on the Web, forming a valid recipe, may be fused at any level-up screen or Pact altar.

The Trade

  • The two slots merge into one; max slots −1 permanently for the run
  • The fused spell inherits both chosen Talents — 2 spells × 3 talents × 3 talents = 9 variants per recipe
  • Fused spells cannot fuse again, but resonate with neighbors at double strength — the scar tissue is sensitive
  • Floor rule: capacity can never shrink below 3 — fusion is unavailable at 3. (Crown of the Hollow Sun's 5-capacity start therefore allows at most two fusions.)
  • Wide Soul exception: Wide Soul fusions compress instead of scar — the freed cell stays usable and the fused cell counts as two spells toward capacity. This is how, and the only way, a Wide Soul fills 8 slots with their 6 hexagonal cells: two fusions → 4 single cells + 2 fused cells = 8 spells

Any two of your Mastered spells that sit adjacent in your region and form a recipe can attune. Recipes involving Holy or Void spells are naturally faction-bound; everything else is open to every sorcerer.

Curse & Radiance — The Twin Risk Engines

Two mirrored five-stage meters that convert play-style into a strategic resource. Curse charges by suffering; Radiance charges by serving. Neither decays on its own.

☾ The Curse Meter (Void School enrollees)

Taking any Void School spell — the Shadow Covenant's special school — manifests the meter (0–100). Each hit taken raises it a fixed +4 (modified by class and relics — not damage size, so chip damage matters and big hits aren't doubly punished). Only specific actions reduce it: a Radiant ally's Dawnbound or Absolution, wisps from Penitent elites, one-use Cleansing Wells, specific relics, and one Pact entity who buys it. Because every Curse outlet except Wells and relics is ally-driven, solo Shadow runs receive a third Cleansing Well and a raised Penitent elite spawn-weight — so the meter stays a managed debt rather than a one-way ratchet when no Radiant ally is present to cleanse.

Power Scaling — The Logarithm and the Ladder

A logarithmic baseline keeps the math sane; engineered step-spikes keep the player leaning forward. The curve is sustainable; the experience is a ladder.

The Baseline (smooth, logarithmic)

SpellDamage(L) = Base × (1 + 0.85 × ln(1 + 0.6L)) // L = spell level 1–10
PlayerPower(t) ≈ Σ spells + artifacts → grows ~ln(t)
EnemyHP(t) = HP₀ × (1 + 0.11t) // linear, t in minutes
EnemyCount(t) = C₀ × (1 + 0.14t) × partyMult // linear
Undertow : budget × 1.03^(sec/10) // Drown only — gentle exponential run-ender

Net effect: per-enemy kill speed slowly declines across a run — pressure always rises — while total screen damage still escalates with spell count, preserving the bullet-heaven spectacle.

The Ladder (the feel)

On top of the smooth curve, the systems deliver discrete steps players can feel and chase. Tuning KPI: at least one step event every 90–120 seconds of a healthy run.

Step Source Magnitude
Lv4 / Lv7 milestones Every spell, twice on the way to 10 Visible behavior jump
Mastery (Lv10 Talent) Player choice A transformation, not a +%
Resonance discovery Clever Web placement Free, instant, earned by thought
Attunement / Eclipse Fusion (slot cost) The largest spikes in the game
Meter stages Curse / Radiance Up to +100%, rented not owned
Pacts & Omens Player-authored deals Spikes with teeth

Afterlight — the vertical climb (where the curve breaks on purpose)

Past 20:00 the logarithmic baseline is deliberately abandoned. Afterlight is a leaderboard mode, and a leaderboard wants a wall, not a plateau. Every 60s survived is a Surge, and each Surge compounds:

EnemyHP ×= 1.18 per Surge // compounding — replaces the linear slope
SpawnBudget ×= 1.12 per Surge // compounding
EliteRate += 2% per Surge (cap 60%) · the Undertow no longer pauses
Crowned elites begin at Surge 3 · a Warden returns every 5th Surge as a checkpoint

Kill-speed falls off a cliff by design; the builds that survive 8–10 Surges are what the board actually measures. Death still pays full Afterlight income, so every Surge climbed is banked — and the v2.1 stacking caps (Tithe of Stars, Patient Glyph) exist precisely so one uncapped outlier can't invalidate this ladder.

Why Not Exponential?

VS-style exponential stacking creates god-mode minutes where input stops mattering — fine for solo idle-fantasy, fatal for 4-player co-op where one god deletes three players' agency. Logarithmic-plus-ladder keeps all four players relevant all run, and makes cooperation itself — cleanses, peels, Eclipse fusion — the highest rung on the ladder.

Enemy Simulation (server, 20 Hz)

  • Spatial hash grid, 8-stud cells — every proximity question (spell hits, auras, contact damage, separation) is a cell lookup, never a full scan
  • Flow-field pathing: one field per player recomputed at 2 Hz; enemies read the field plus a boids-lite separation force. Hordes flow around walls without per-enemy pathfinding
  • LOD tiers: enemies within 80 studs of any player simulate at 20 Hz; beyond that, 5 Hz. Cosmetic crowd entities (the back of the horde) are client-only and despawn into real enemies as they approach
  • Spell hits are analytic, not physical: every spell shape is a math query (circle, sector, line, ring) against the hash grid. No Touched events, no physics projectiles — ~900 logical projectiles cost a fraction of one physics solver step

Networking & Replication

  • Enemy state → clients: packed buffers over unreliable remotes at 10 Hz (position as int16 grid offsets + state byte); clients interpolate with 100ms delay. ~150 enemies ≈ 1.5 KB per tick per client
  • Spell casts: server sends castId + seed + origin once; clients play the entire deterministic VFX locally from the seed. No per-projectile replication, ever
  • Damage numbers / kill feed: batched once per 0.25s; aggregated ("×14") past a threshold
  • Player movement uses Roblox's native character replication (client-authoritative) with server sanity checks — the one concession to responsiveness, fenced by validation below

Anti-Cheat (server-authoritative everything)

  • Clients never report damage, kills, XP, or pickups — the server computes all of it; the client is a renderer with a joystick
  • Movement validation: speed cap ×1.2 tolerance, teleport detection with snap-back; dash windows tracked server-side
  • Choice validation: level-up picks, Web placements, pact accepts, and fusions are requests the server validates against its own run state (right card? legal slot? both players actually Mastered?)
  • Rate limits on all remotes; malformed payloads disconnect silently

Hardcore Mode — The Unbroken Night

One life, no nets. An opt-in challenge layer that adds no new content to learn and no power to grind — it takes every system the game already has and turns the safety off. Rewards are prestige only.

The standard run is built around recovery: auto-revives, cleansing wells, rerolls, the Last Light pause. Hardcore is the same night with the recovery removed. The twin risk meters were designed for exactly this — Curse that can only spiral and Radiance that paints you for the whole map finally have nowhere to hide. You arm the mode by lighting the Unbroken flame — interacting with a black-glass brazier in the Threshold, the half-lit hub sanctum where every run is prepared and launched (§24). Doing so before you descend flags that run as hardcore; there is no toggling it off once you drop in, and when your flame goes out — your last life spent — the run is over for good.

Entry & Eligibility

  • Unlocked per faction after your first Sovereign kill on that faction — never the new-player experience
  • Opt-in per run at the black-glass brazier in the Threshold (the hub, §24); lighting it sets that single run to hardcore, and the choice is made before the run, never mid-run
  • Co-op is all-or-nothing: every player in the lobby must light the flame. Hardcore matchmaking pools separately — no mixed safe/unsafe parties
  • Your meta-progress still counts. Research, classes, and unlocked relics carry in — hardcore isn't a fresh save, it's the same power against a harder night. The variable is danger, not grind
  • All five zones and Afterlight support hardcore; Unbroken Afterlight is the game's apex board

Death, Downs & the Party

  • One life, full stop. There is no Revive Pool, no ally channel, no auto-revive, no second wind — and Phoenix Rebirth Pact and Radiance Vigil cannot raise the dead here. The instant you go down you are out of the run
  • In co-op a fallen player becomes a pure spectator spirit: they can still ping to help the living and the run goes on for whoever remains, but they cannot be brought back by anyone
  • When the last player falls, the run is over — no Last Light reprieve. Solo, that is your first down; in a party, the moment the final survivor drops
  • Death still pays. Hardcore banks prestige at the depth you reached — pushing a doomed Unbroken run is always worth it, the same death-as-income philosophy as the standard mode

The Seal of the Unbroken — rewards & prestige

  • Currency: hardcore pays standard Dawnmotes / Nightdrops at ×1.5, plus a prestige-only token — Embers of the Unbroken (~6–10 per Unbroken clear, +1 per Afterlight Surge)
  • Embers buy cosmetics only — a black-flame halo / shadow-trail, the Unbroken vestment, and a hub brazier that lights one ring per zone cleared hardcore — 40–150 Embers each. Never power, and never buyable with Robux: prestige must be earned. The same cosmetics-only line the whole economy holds — in a clone-saturated platform, trust is the product
  • Separate leaderboards per zone / class / party size, plus the single apex Unbroken Afterlight (Surges survived in hardcore overtime)
  • Profile Seals: a first hardcore Sovereign kill stamps a permanent seal on that faction's codex. Earn both faction seals to unlock Eclipse Unbroken — the soloist phantom-seam of the Locket of the Other Side, re-lit in black flame. The game's ultimate flex, and still purely cosmetic
  • Run scorecards mark hardcore clears with a cracked-moon seal, distinct from standard clears
Design Intent: Sharpen the Dials, Add Nothing

Hardcore deliberately introduces zero new spells, enemies, or power. It only re-tunes the existing balance dials to their sharpest setting and removes the recovery systems — which is why it can ship the day the game does and never fragments the design. Keeping its rewards cosmetic-only is load-bearing: the moment hardcore grants power, it becomes a treadmill and the genre-trust thesis breaks. The mode exists for mastery and bragging rights, full stop — and it is the truest test of the two risk meters the whole game is built around.
